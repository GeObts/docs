If you're new to mining or on-chain, these are the words that come up most.

## Mining

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="ASIC">
    Application-Specific Integrated Circuit. A chip designed to do one thing — in this case, hash Bitcoin's SHA-256 algorithm. ASICs are vastly more efficient than GPUs or CPUs at this work.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Hashrate">
    How much hashing your miner does per second. Measured in H/s, KH/s, MH/s, GH/s, TH/s, PH/s, EH/s. A modern home ASIC does TH/s (terahashes per second). The whole Bitcoin network does ~600 EH/s.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Stratum">
    The standard protocol miners use to talk to pools. When you point your ASIC at `stratum+tcp://...`, you're using stratum.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Worker">
    A label for one mining device. If you run three ASICs, each is a worker. They share the same BTC payout address but have different worker names so you can tell them apart on the dashboard.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Share">
    A proof-of-work submission from a miner. Each share is a hash that meets the pool's difficulty target (lower than Bitcoin's network difficulty). The pool counts shares to attribute work fairly.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Block">
    A block is what miners actually find. When the pool finds one, it earns the block subsidy (currently 3.125 BTC) plus transaction fees, and distributes that to contributors.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Pool fee">
    A small percentage the pool keeps from each block reward to cover infrastructure and development.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Solo vs pool mining">
    Solo: you mine alone and keep 100% of any block you find — but small miners may go years without finding one. Pool: you join a group, your share counts toward the pool's collective work, and you get steady small payouts.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Bitcoin

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="BTC">
    The native asset of the Bitcoin network. What miners earn. What the pool pays out.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Block subsidy / block reward">
    The fixed BTC amount minted with each new block. Currently 3.125 BTC. Halves roughly every four years.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Difficulty">
    A network parameter that adjusts how hard it is to find a valid block, automatically retargeting every 2016 blocks (~2 weeks) to keep block time near 10 minutes.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## On-chain (Base / EVM)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Base">
    Coinbase's L2 — a fast, cheap blockchain that settles to Ethereum. BasedMining's on-chain side runs on Base.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Base Sepolia">
    The testnet version of Base. Used for testing without real money. Get test ETH from any Base Sepolia faucet.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Wallet">
    A piece of software (or hardware) that holds your private keys. Examples: Rainbow, Coinbase Wallet, MetaMask, Frame. You need one to interact with on-chain BasedMining.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="NFT">
    Non-Fungible Token. A unique on-chain record. A BasedPool NFT represents your participation share in the pool.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Gas">
    The fee you pay to make any transaction on an EVM chain. Base gas is very cheap (often less than a cent on mainnet, free on testnet faucets).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Mint">
    The act of creating a new on-chain item — in our case, a BasedPool NFT. You pay the mint price + gas, the contract creates the NFT and assigns it to your wallet.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Claim">
    Withdrawing the rewards your NFT has accrued. A wallet transaction that transfers the rewards into your address.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Agents and payments

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="x402">
    An open HTTP standard for paying for API calls with stablecoins. The server returns HTTP 402, the client pays, the call completes. See [x402.org](https://www.x402.org).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Stablecoin">
    A token pegged to a fiat currency (typically USD). USDC on Base is the canonical example.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Agent">
    Here, an autonomous program (often LLM-driven) that can take actions, including mining decisions and payments, without human intervention per call.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
